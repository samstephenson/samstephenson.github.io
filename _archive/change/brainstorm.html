Offer an Incentive.

Incentives

Either carrot or stick, incentives can be powerful motivators, but treat them with caution. They’re often an ugly solution and can taint the relationship with your audience.

Consider

  • Can you tempt them with something of value?
  • Would it help to punish bad behaviour?

Make them commit.

Commitments

We seek to live up to our promises.

Consider

  • Give them an opportunity to make a commitment. The more effort, the more at stake, the more publicly announced, the better.
  • Could you give them a challenge to overcome and feel good about?
  • Can you provide a cause to publicly align with?

Single them out from the crowd.

Pluralistic Ignorance

We’ll often follow others and become blind to alternatives, especially when drawbacks aren’t obvious, or if matters are aren’t discussed by the group.

Consider

  • Single them out from the crowd, and communicate with them individually.
  • Are they simply following a crowd? How might you single them out?

Make yours the path of least resistance.

Defaults

We mostly go with the flow of default options given to us..

Consider

  • Choose your default option carefully.
  • Is the right choice easier to make than the wrong choice?

Reduce the number of options.

Choice Overload

Too many choices create stress and uncertainty, reducing our ability to think rationally. In this case we’ll often fall back to the default option, or blindly follow those around us.

Consider

  • Having fewer options allows less chance of mistakes and faster decisions.

Speak through a voice they'll respect.

Messenger bias

We’re influenced heavily by who communicates the information we receive, placing irrational trust in ‘experts’ and other figures or symbols of authority.

Consider

  • Does the voice communicating your message have authority in the eyes of your audience?
  • Who would they listen to or trust the most?
  • Ask for something that sounds less intimidating or difficult.

Give, generously, unexpectedly.

Reciprocity

A natural desire for fairness compels us to reciprocate good deeds.

Consider

  • Be the first in the relationship to give. Make it generous and unexpected. You’ll have open ears and good will in return. More often than not, they’ll give back too.

Give the impression of scarcity.

Scarcity

We value things more when they are scarce, or only available to a select group. We hate to feel like we’re missing out.

Consider

  • Could you play hard to get?
  • Can your product feel exclusive in some way to your audience?
  • Can you instill a fear of missing out?

Arouse them.

Affect

We are prey to our emotions and temptations. Hormones and endorphins can have a significant effect on how we process decisions.

Consider

  • Could you make them happy, sad, angry, horny, hungry, thirsty? ...Would this make things easier for you?
  • Consider all the senses available.

Show them what they stand to lose.

Loss aversion

Fear of loss is a powerful motivator. The pain of losing something is roughly twice as strong as the pleasure of gaining another at the same value.

Consider

  • What do they stand to lose by not doing what you’re asking?
  • Can you frame financial incentives in terms of potential loss?

Be likeable.

Liking

It’s best to establish liking, before getting down to business. People are more receptive to messages from people and brands they actually like.

Consider

  • Does your audience like you? If no, what could you do to change this?
  • If your brand were a person. Would people actually like you?

Give the payoff now, let them pay later.

Hyperbolic Discounting

We have an overwhelming preference for immediate payoffs over later ones, even if we’d be getting a better deal in the long run.

Consider

  • Can you separate the hard decision from its consequences?
  • Can you reward them sooner?
  • Can you delay the painful bit until later?

Use others as a good example.

Social Norms

We tend to follow what those around us are doing, especially in situations of uncertainty.

Consider

  • Can you highlight positive things people like them are doing?
  • Can you set an example for them to follow?
  • Could they do it together, with the people close to them?

Repeat, repeat, repeat until it becomes normal.

Status Quo Bias

The more we repeat behaviours, the more automatic they become. Eventually they’ll become habituated defaults.

Consider

  • Can you get them to repeat the desired behaviour several times, to begin to habituate it?
  • Can you interrupt them before bad habits form?

Salience

Our attention is drawn to what seems novel and relevant to us. Surprising, funny, contrasting, different, new etc.

Consider

  • Will they notice you at the critical moment?

Give short, frequent and memorable reminders.

Availability

An event’s memorableness is measured by the strength of our emotional reaction, how frequently we’ve been exposed to it, and how recently it occured.

Consider

  • How might recent or powerful memories be affecting their decision making?
  • Tell a powerful story that will stick in the audience’s mind.

Use what's already familiar.

Familiarity Bias

We have a natural preference for things familiar to us.

Consider

  • Does your desired behaviour feel familiar?
  • Can you relate the desired behaviour to something the user is already familiar with?
  • Let the user get used to you quickly, make them feel at home.
  • Can you build on existing habits?

Associate with the desirable.

Halo Effect

Our feelings about one thing ‘spill over’ into other things related or nearby. We’ll make generalisations about the whole based on the perceived quality of one part of it.

Consider

  • How are surrounding products or messages affecting your own?
  • Would associating with certain others improve their perception of you?

End on a positive note.

Peak/End Rule

We remember experiences as a highlight reel of the most memorable bits, and the ending.

Consider

  • Pay attention to the end of the customer’s experience. Make it good, it counts for more.
  • How might you create small, memorable moments within your experience?

Save the most important step until last.

Post Completion Error

When our main task is finished, we often forget things related to previous steps.

Consider

  • Make sure they’ve completed the task, before giving any reward.
  • Once they have what they want, are they likely to ignore extra steps?

Ego

We like to act in ways that make us feel better about ourselves.

Consider

  • How might your product / brand make the audience feel good about themselves?

Don't lay the blame.

Fundamental Attribution

We tend to accept good outcomes as our own doing, while placing blame on others or things out of our control.

Consider

  • If they’re doing something wrong, no point arguing it’s their fault. Focus on positive forward action instead.
  • Let them feel responsible for success.

Willpower

Like a muscle, our willpower tires with use. Don’t rely on it for long-term change. It only works in short bursts.

  • How could you remove the need for willpower... Automation? Setting up defaults?

Ask for a short, fixed-term commitment.

Short Commitments

A fixed period is easier to commit to and can establish habits that lead to long-term change.

Consider

  • Are you asking too much?
  • Could you set a fixed period for change, rather than indefinite commitment?
  • Ask for something that sounds less intimidating or difficult.

Focus on the desired behaviour.

Positive Reinforcement

Saying ‘don’t do x’ can actually reinforce a bad behaviour. Focus on the behaviour you want, avoid bringing up the one you don’t.

Consider

  • Don’t focus on stopping old behaviours, create new ones instead.
  • Are you speaking in positive terms?

Triggers

No behaviour happens without a trigger.

  • If {trigger} then {your behaviour}.

Give feedback. Quickly and constructively.

Feedback

Without feedback, we’ll never know whether what we’re doing is right or wrong.

Consider

  • Let them know immediately when they’ve done the right thing.
  • Show the user they’ve been recognised.
  • Every action should have a perceptible effect.

Keep them coming back for more with a variable reward.

Variable Rewards

Like facebook’s little red notification badge, rewards that vary in frequency and size are much more addictive then those that are predictable and regular.

Consider

  • Is your reward so regular they’ve come to take it for granted?
  • Do they have to be successful every time?

Anchoring

Value is often set by anchors or imprints in our minds which we use as mental reference points.

Consider

  • Could you ask more than is actually required of them?

Leave clues to put them in the right frame of mind.

Priming

Our acts are often influenced by cues around us picked up by our sub-conscious.

Consider

  • What cues could you place around them to steer them in the right direction? Consider all the senses.
  • How might you get them in the right frame of mind before they interact with you?
